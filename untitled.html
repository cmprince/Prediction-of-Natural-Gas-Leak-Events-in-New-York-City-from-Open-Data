Abstract

The dangers of natural gas distribution and aging infrastructure are well known, but determining the risk for incidents related to natural gas is a difficult statistical, engineering, and civic challenge. Furthermore, investigating and repairing infrastructure can be prohibitively expensive. We sought to create predictive models that could prioritize future in-person inspections and develop a risk profile of characteristics of the built environment regarding gas leaks. We did this using only publicly-available data and compared several statistical methods to identify an optimal predictive model. We found that given a fairly broad approach to feature selection, a naive prediction based only on historical leaks performed better than more complex models, such as regularized linear regression, random forests, and multi-layer perceptron neural networks. These more complex models did show promise; however, regarding their ability to detect complex structures in the data, improve feature selection and possibly address more precisely defined objectives, such as prioritizing only select geographic areas.

Introduction

In New York City, there were more than 60,000 emergency calls made by the New York City Fire Department (FDNY) related to gas leaks between 2013 and 2015. While most of these calls resulted in no damage or injury, several incidents did end with fatalities. Manhattan leaks three to five times more natural gas than cities with newer infrastructure \citep{gallagher_natural_2015} and of the 6,400 miles of gas main lines running under New York City's streets, 53% were installed prior to 1960. In 2012, Con Edison experienced 83 leaks for every 100 miles of main. Furthermore, replacing a main in NYC can cost from $2.2 million to $8 million per mile, so prioritizing investment is critical \citep{forman_caution_2014}

There have been successful attempts to use statistical analysis on large datasets to assess building risk in general, not just gas leaks. In 2013, the New York City Mayor's Office of Data Analytics (MODA) worked with the FDNY to improve its Risk Based Inspection System. The system is designed to prioritize the inspections of buildings in the jurisdiction of FDNY so as to aid in the detection of severe violations. Prior MODA's involvement, the FDNY model was based on limited data about the city's buildings and weighted simply by anecdotal evidence from firefighters. The improved model uses a statistical regression based on additional building datasets to increase the efficacy of inspections. The model performance went from not being much better than random selection to finding nearly three-quarters of severe violations in just the first one-quarter of inspections. \citep{nyc_moda_nyc_2013}
In a similar fashion, we wish to determine if regression, or another machine learning method, can be used to predict the locations of natural gas leaks as reported to FDNY.  We investigate if an effective predictive model for NYC natural gas leaks can be developed using only publicly available datasets. We use multiple data selection strategies and machine learning methods to develop multiple models. We compared the model performance with regard to its ability to predict 2015 gas leaks based upon 2013-2014 data and we identified a few challenges and opportunities for further research.

A conventional statistical analysis might focus on correlational relationships, but that is complicated when there is a large number of features and feature selection is non-trivial. Our research intuition is that building predictive models certainly wouldn't address cause and may not shed deep insight into correlational factors for gas leaks, but could add value as applied to prioritizing more deterministic methods, such as on-site inspections. These probabilistic models would prove their worth in terms of accuracy and add value by optimizing the inspection effort. Additionally, as predictive models become more powerful and research with this dataset continues, a predictive model approach could lend insight into important correlations that might be used to develop risk profiles for characteristics of the built environment, such as building type.
